# GTM Story Bank: real voices + shoot-ready micro-dramas

**Written:** 6 July 2026. Companion to `GTM-PLAYBOOK.md` and `GTM-AUDIENCE-RESEARCH.md`.
Purpose: turn the audience research into creative fuel, real candidate voices, and
micro-drama concepts you can actually shoot. The rule stays: sell the struggle and
the feeling, never the feature.

**Note on the quotes below:** these are real first-person accounts pulled from
public sources. Use them as *proof the emotions are real* and as inspiration for
tone, don't lift a specific person's words verbatim in an ad or imply endorsement
without permission. For production, pull 5-10 more from LinkedIn "how I passed
CAMS" posts and Reddit r/moneylaundering exam threads.

---

## 1. Real voices (the emotional truth, sourced)

**Tatiana Welch, first-person LinkedIn account of passing CAMS:**
- *"A week before the exam, doubts began to creep in. Was I ready? Had I studied
  hard enough?"*
- On exam day: *"Negative thoughts kept surfacing, but I acknowledged them and
  asked them to leave."* (a song was her emotional anchor)
- The exam: flagged over half the questions; 45 minutes left; *"the questions were
  designed to be tricky, and attention to detail was crucial."*
- The result: saw **PASS**, score 84, *"I literally burst into tears. I was so
  proud of myself, I did it!"*

**camsprep, "why 4 in 10 fail" (the failure reality):**
- The trap: memorisers hit a wall *"when faced with a scenario question requiring
  judgment about why a control is appropriate."*
- **75-80% of the exam is scenario/reasoning**, not recall; the multi-select
  format gives **zero credit for an incomplete answer** and most people meet it
  for the first time on exam day, *"no partial credit safety net."*
- On dumps: real exam content *"were reasoning-based and bore little resemblance to
  commonly circulated dump content."*
- The cost of failing: ~4 in 10 retake; each retake costs time, fees, and
  *"employer patience"*; waiting periods stack 30 / 60 / 90 days.
- The line that is basically Enso's whole pitch, from an expert:
  *"The candidates who tell me they found the exam easier than expected are always
  the ones who prepared for judgment calls, not answer recall."*

**The universal emotional arc** (use it as the spine of every story):
> Doubt (a week before) → the grind squeezed into a full life → the exam that
> tricks the memoriser and rewards the decider → tears of relief, or the quiet
> shame of a retake.

## 2. The campaign spine (one truth, told many ways)

Everything above says the same thing, and it is the one message that is true,
differentiating, and emotional at once:

> **The exam doesn't test what you memorised. It tests whether you'd make the right
> call. Dumps train recall. We train judgment. Know you're ready before you walk
> in.**

That is not our marketing claim, it is what the candidates and the failure data
already say. We just say it back to them.

## 3. Shoot-ready micro-dramas (hook → beats → turn → end-card)

Each is 30-60 seconds, dramatized, cut for Shorts/Reels/TikTok/LinkedIn, mapped to
a cluster from the audience research. End-card is always the same one CTA: the free
simulation.

### A. "The week before" (universal; skews Cluster 3, the Western veteran)
- **Hook (0-3s):** a composed professional, in command by day. Hard cut to 11pm,
  alone at the kitchen table, laptop glow. Text: *"A week before the exam, the
  doubts came."*
- **Beats:** flashcards; a tricky practice question she gets wrong; the whisper,
  *"Was I ready? Had I studied enough?"* Fear on a competent face.
- **Turn:** the exam hall. She flags half the questions. 45 minutes left. One
  breath. She trusts it.
- **Payoff:** the screen says **PASS**. She bursts into tears.
- **End-card:** *"The exam doesn't test what you memorised. It tests whether you're
  ready. Know before you walk in."* Enso. First simulation free.

### B. "200 questions" (Cluster 1, the Gulf expat, your Dubai-tutor story)
- **Hook:** a man in a Dubai tower, phone buzzes. A tutor: *"200 questions.
  Memorise these. Trust me."*
- **Beats:** he memorises on the metro, at midnight, mouthing answers; a photo of
  his family back home on the desk, this job feeds them.
- **Turn:** exam day. First question is a scenario: *"why is this control
  appropriate?"* It is not on his list. His 200 answers do not fit. The room goes
  quiet around him.
- **Payoff line:** *"The exam didn't ask what he memorised. It asked what he'd do."*
- **End-card:** *"You didn't move three thousand miles to gamble your career on 200
  questions. Learn to decide."* Enso.

### C. "Half a year's salary" (Cluster 2, the South Asian banker)
- **Hook:** a young banker looks at the exam fee on his screen. Text: *"This exam
  costs half my year's salary."*
- **Beats:** he counts it out; tells his parents; the weight of the bet. Studying
  after a full day, eyes heavy.
- **Turn:** the fear, said plainly: *"If I fail, it's gone. And I'm still here."*
- **Payoff:** because he learned to decide, he walks in steady, passes; the quiet
  pride at home, or the call offering the posting abroad.
- **End-card:** *"When the exam costs half your salary, you can't afford to guess."*
  Enso.

### D. "Two candidates" (the anti-dump truth; converts across clusters)
- **Built from the real quote:** the ones who found it easy prepared for judgment,
  not recall.
- **Hook:** split screen, same exam, two people. One memorised 500 answers. One
  learned to make the call.
- **Beats:** the scenario question lands. The memoriser freezes, sweats, guesses.
  The decider reads it, thinks, chooses.
- **Payoff:** the memoriser flags everything and runs out of time; the decider puts
  the pen down early.
- **End-card:** *"Memorise 500 answers, or learn to make one call. The exam only
  asks the second."* Enso.

### E. "The frontier" (Cluster 5, the CCAS crypto pioneer)
- **Hook:** an analyst staring at a blockchain explorer, hundreds of hops, lost.
  Text: *"Crypto crime has no textbook."*
- **Beats:** the field moves faster than the rules; the quiet fear of faking it in
  a room full of people who assume you know.
- **Turn/payoff:** a clear map appears (the course); she follows the money, and for
  the first time she *understands* it.
- **End-card:** *"Crypto crime has no textbook. So we wrote it. The only real CCAS
  course on the internet."* Enso.

## 4. Production notes

- **One CTA, always:** the free full exam simulation. The story creates the fear;
  the readiness score is the salesman.
- **Face + honesty:** end at least some films on the founder ("I built this
  because the gap between the practice and the real exam is brutal"). Compliance
  buyers trust a person.
- **AI production stack** makes these cheap and fast (this environment has
  image/video/voice generation wired in, Higgsfield). Prototype one before buying
  tools.
- **Keep pulling real stories:** the richest source material is public first-person
  "how I passed / how I failed CAMS" posts. Each is a script. Two are quoted above;
  gather ten.
- **Never dramatize a lie:** no fake urgency, no invented pass rates. The audience
  audits claims for a living; honesty is the warmth.
