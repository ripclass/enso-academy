## Scene 2 — cold open `Cold open: the closed matter` (NEW quiz, 1 Q, correct = b)
intro: "No teaching yet. The board announcement is drafted and waiting on your sign-off. Commit."

prompt: "You take over financial crime at a US national bank. Eighteen months of AML program findings have just been resolved: the OCC, the bank's examiner, has signed a consent order with a remediation plan, and the board wants to announce that 'the regulatory matter is now closed.' Your read?"

- a: "It is closed: the OCC is the bank's chartering regulator and examiner, the consent order is the operative instrument on exactly these findings, and no other federal agency re-litigates a matter the primary supervisor has resolved on its merits."
- b (KEYED): "It is one seat at the table: the OCC has priced the bank's condition, but FinCEN holds an independent civil claim on the same program failures, willfulness in the record would take it criminal, and other hooks may be live. Confirm who else is looking."
- c: "It is closed for the bank but not the holding company: the remaining exposure runs to the Federal Reserve at the consolidated level, and a parallel holding-company order will complete the regulatory picture for the group."
- d: "It is provisionally closed: US agencies operate a lead-regulator convention under which the first resolution bars duplicative claims on the same facts, so another agency may act only if the OCC reopens or refers its own order."

explanation: "Name the reflex: one regulator, one resolution, one announcement. The American system does not work that way, because there is no single AML regulator to close a matter with. The OCC consent order prices exactly one thing, the examined institution's condition, and it leaves standing FinCEN's independent civil authority over the same program failures, the Department of Justice's criminal lane if the record shows the failures were willful, OFAC's separate claim if any sanctions dimension surfaces, and a state seat if a licence gives one leverage. The holding-company answer names a real seat but promotes it to the only one. The lead-regulator convention does not exist: no US agency's resolution bars another's independent statutory claim, which is why resolutions in this system are assembled, not issued. Before anyone says 'closed', the disciplined move is the map: classify the conduct, list every authority whose hook it touches, and confirm each seat's posture. The lesson ahead hands you that map, and the deep case shows all five seats eating from a single course of conduct."

## Scene 3 — `The decision standard` (NEW slide, callout)
item: "There is no single AML regulator; the map is the competence. Classify the conduct and the table seats itself: willfulness goes criminal, to DOJ; program and reporting failures as such go to FinCEN's civil lane; the examined institution's condition goes to its chartering examiner; sanctions conduct goes to OFAC; licence leverage goes to the states. US resolutions are assembled, not issued: settling one seat never closes the table."

narration: "Here is the standard behind the call you just made, and every decision in this lesson reapplies it. The United States has no single anti-money-laundering regulator; it has a table, and the discipline is classification. Ask what the conduct is, and the seats fill themselves. Willful conduct goes criminal: the Department of Justice, with the laundering statutes, the BSA's own criminal provision, and the sanctions crimes. Program and reporting failures as such stay civil: that is FinCEN's lane as the Bank Secrecy Act's administrator. The condition of an examined institution belongs to whoever charters and examines it, and that authority's consent order, not anyone's fine, is what runs the remediation. Sanctions conduct belongs to OFAC on its own statute, coordinating when the same facts go criminal. And where a licence gives a state leverage, the state takes a seat too. One more rule completes the standard: these claims are independent, so a resolution here is assembled across the table, never issued from one chair, and the practitioner's question is never 'which regulator' but 'which regulators, and who leads'. Hold that frame; the toolkit, the agency map and the deep case all reapply it."

### Step 1 `Evidence item 1: the conversion question` (id 'conversion', key a)
evidence.observed: "Two strands of conduct are on the table. Strand one: HSBC Bank USA, N.A. provided correspondent services to the group's Mexican affiliate without adequate monitoring, and at least USD 881 million in cartel proceeds moved through; the OCC's examinations had flagged the program failures for years. Strand two: non-US affiliates processed US-dollar payments for parties in Iran, Cuba, Libya, Sudan and Burma through altered messages."
evidence.source: "The public record of the resolution documents."
evidence.inference: "Program failures resolve civilly at FinCEN and the OCC every year. The first question on this file is what, if anything, takes strand one somewhere else."
evidence.confidence: "High; both conduct strands and the examination history are documented findings of the resolution record."
decision.prompt: "Strand one is an AML program failure, and program failures are ordinarily civil matters. What converted this one into federal criminal charges?"
- a (KEYED): "Willfulness: the bank persisted through years of flagged examination findings, and knowledge plus persistence converts program and due-diligence failures from civil matters into crimes under the BSA's own criminal provision. The facts, not the failure type, made it criminal."
- b: "Scale: at USD 881 million in suspect flows the amount crossed the level at which the statute directs criminal rather than civil disposition, and a program failure carrying nine-figure volumes goes to the Department of Justice as a matter of course."
- c: "Contamination: the sanctions strand made the whole matter criminal, because once sanctions charges attach to one strand of an institution's conduct, every related compliance failure in the same institution is swept into the criminal resolution with it."
- d: "Referral: the OCC's examiners referred their findings, and a prudential referral is itself what converts an examination matter into a criminal case, since the Department of Justice does not open BSA matters without the examining agency's certification."
decision.explanation: "The BSA has two faces, and the line between them is the most practical distinction in US enforcement: its regulatory provisions ground civil penalties for failures as such, while its criminal provision reaches willful violations, and willfulness is built from what the institution knew and did anyway. Years of flagged findings with the failures persisting is that pattern. The scale answer invents a threshold: no dollar level converts a civil matter to a criminal one, and nine-figure programs have resolved civilly where knowledge was absent. The contamination answer misreads the strands: the sanctions charges stood on their own statutes, and the BSA counts had to earn their criminal character separately. And the referral answer reverses the machinery: examiners refer matters constantly, but a referral carries no legal conversion; the Department of Justice charges what the evidence of state of mind supports, certificate or none."
reveal: "The Department of Justice took the criminal lead on both strands: a four-count felony information, willful BSA program and correspondent due-diligence failures on strand one, sanctions violations on strand two. The willfulness was the door. What walked through it is the next card."

### Step 2 `Evidence item 2: the table assembles` (id 'assembly', key c)
evidence.observed: "With the criminal lead set, the other authorities position their claims: the OCC as the national bank's examiner, the Federal Reserve above the holding company, FinCEN as the BSA's administrator, OFAC on the sanctions strand. The chief financial officer fears a pile-on: five authorities, five separate penalties, each priced independently on the same facts."
evidence.source: "The public record of the resolution documents."
evidence.inference: "Five hooks are real. Whether five hooks mean five stacked penalties is the question this step decides."
evidence.confidence: "High; each authority's claim and instrument are documented in the resolution record."
decision.prompt: "Is the CFO right that the claims will simply stack?"
- a: "Yes: each authority holds an independent statutory claim, independence means independent pricing, and the total is whatever the five separate penalties sum to, which is why US resolutions dwarf single-regulator jurisdictions on identical facts."
- b: "No: the authorities negotiate one global figure first and then divide it among themselves by an allocation formula, so the number of agencies at the table changes the split but never the size of the total the institution pays."
- c (KEYED): "No: the claims are independent but the pricing coordinates. Penalties on the same facts run concurrent where lanes overlap, and settlements credit payments across agencies, one payment deemed to satisfy another, so the assembly avoids double-counting by design rather than by accident."
- d: "Yes, unless the institution elects a single-agency global settlement: US practice lets the respondent choose one lead agency whose resolution then extinguishes the parallel claims, in exchange for a premium on that agency's penalty."
decision.explanation: "Independence and coordination are both true, and the craft is in how they combine. Each authority charges its own statutory lane, which is what makes the table impossible to escape; but the pricing coordinates, with concurrent civil penalties where two agencies price the same failures and satisfaction crediting where one payment answers two claims. The stacking answer misses the coordination and would make every multi-agency matter arithmetically explosive. The global-figure answer invents a formula that does not exist: there is no central allocator, only agency-to-agency coordination. And the election answer imagines a choice the respondent never has: no US agency's resolution extinguishes another's claim, and no premium buys that result, which is exactly why counsel negotiates with the whole table at once."
reveal: "The assembly, as documented: FinCEN's civil money penalty ran concurrent with the OCC's; OFAC's settlement on the sanctions strand was satisfied by the Department of Justice forfeiture. One conduct, five hooks, no double-counting by accident: the resolution was assembled, not issued."

### Step 3 `Evidence item 3: what runs the bank afterward` (id 'aftermath', key b; PREDICT the operational instrument)
evidence.observed: "The package is about to be announced: a deferred prosecution agreement with a forfeiture that will headline every story, civil money penalties across the agencies, a sanctions settlement, and remediation obligations. The board asks a forward-looking question: for the next five years, which piece of this actually governs how the bank runs?"
evidence.source: "The public record of the resolution documents."
evidence.inference: "Fines are paid once. Something in this package will shape hiring, systems, testing and reporting for years. Predict which piece."
evidence.confidence: "High; the operative instruments and their terms are the binding public record."
decision.prompt: "Before the reveal: which instrument governs the bank's daily life for the next five years?"
- a: "The deferred prosecution agreement's forfeiture terms: a payment of that size is financed, provisioned and reported over years, and its instalment schedule becomes the binding calendar around which the bank's remediation and budgets are actually planned."
- b (KEYED): "The supervisory layer: the examiner's consent order and the deferred prosecution agreement's monitorship, because supervision, not prosecution, runs remediation, and those are the instruments that reach into systems, staffing, testing and reporting month after month."
- c: "FinCEN's penalty conditions: as the BSA's administrator, FinCEN attaches the ongoing program obligations to its civil money penalty, and its periodic certifications become the operating rhythm the bank answers to until the penalty conditions lapse."
- d: "The OFAC settlement's compliance commitments: sanctions exposure is the strand with strict-liability stakes, so the sanctions commitments carry the most intrusive ongoing obligations and the most frequent external verification of the package."
decision.explanation: "Follow what each instrument can actually do. A forfeiture is money: paid, absorbed, and finished as an operational matter however large the number. FinCEN's penalty prices violations as such; it does not station anyone inside the bank. The OFAC settlement resolves the sanctions strand's civil liability; its commitments matter but do not run the institution. The instruments with hands are the supervisory ones: a consent order carries findings, deadlines and examiner follow-up, and a monitorship installs a standing observer whose reports go to the prosecutor, which together reach into systems, hiring, testing and reporting continuously. The durable rule: fines headline, supervision governs, and counsel who negotiates the consent order's terms carelessly has traded the bank's next five years for a better press release."
reveal: "The package: a deferred prosecution agreement with USD 1.256 billion in forfeiture and a five-year monitorship; a USD 500 million OCC civil money penalty with a consent order; USD 165 million from the Federal Reserve at the holding-company level; FinCEN's penalty concurrent with the OCC's; and OFAC's USD 375 million settlement, satisfied by the forfeiture. Total: USD 1.921 billion, with New York's district attorney participating in the forfeiture besides. And the pieces that changed the bank's daily life were exactly the quiet ones: the consent orders and the monitor governed the remediation for years."

debrief: "Read the map back with the three rules it fixes. First, jurisdiction follows function, not size: each authority charged exactly its statutory lane, criminal willfulness, civil program failure, prudential condition, holding-company supervision, sanctions, and those lanes are stable across every major resolution since. Second, the prudential layer is the operational one: fines headline, but the consent order and the monitor are what changed the bank's daily life for five years, supervision, not prosecution, runs remediation. Third, the assembly is the deterrent: the system's plurality, often criticised as fragmentation, is in enforcement terms a force multiplier, because an institution cannot settle its way out with one agency while four others hold independent claims on the same facts."
