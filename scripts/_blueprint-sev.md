- a: "Release it. Every party screens clean and the documents conform, so the sanctions question has been answered; continuing to hold a conforming payment is second-guessing a clean control result you have no specific basis to doubt."
- b: "Hold only pending a list hit or evidence of intent. Absent a designated party or any proof that someone knew of a sanctions nexus, there is nothing actionable here, and indicators no one has actually found are not violations you can act on."
- c (CORRECT): "Do not release yet. In this corridor, a result this clean is a finding, not a clearance, because evasion is built to screen clean. Work the three disguises, who, where and what, and the joins between your own evidence surfaces before you dispose of it."

explanation: "Notice what 'clean' actually measured: that no party you happened to check appears on a list you happened to hold. Release-on-clean is the single most expensive error in this module, the one every deep case ahead punishes, because it treats list screening as if it were sanctions compliance. The intent objection is the other trap: US civil sanctions liability is strict, so you do not need proof that anyone knew, and the indicators you have not yet looked for would be your own institution's records once you did. ## Scene 3 — `The decision standard` (NEW slide, callout)
item: "Evasion is the mirror of your controls. A sanctioned actor who needs the system must pass three screens, so it disguises one of three things: WHO (unlisted names, fronts, nominees), WHERE (geography laundered through third countries, vessels and documents), and WHAT (value converted into commodities, gold or tokens). A clean result is what a competent disguise produces, so a clean screen in a high-risk context is a finding, not a clearance. No single flag convicts; evasion is found at the joins, message against document, document against vessel track, company against its own story."

narration: "Here is the standard behind the call you just made, and every decision in this lesson reapplies it. Start from the adversary: a sanctioned actor who needs your rails has studied the three screens the last lesson built, so it does not defeat them, it dresses to pass them. There are only three things to disguise. The who, so the listed party never appears, hidden behind unlisted fronts and nominees. The where, so the geography is laundered, through third countries, transfers at sea, falsified papers. The what, so the value is converted into oil, gold or tokens whose provenance can be rewritten. That is why a clean result proves nothing on its own: clean is exactly what a good disguise is built to produce, so in a high-risk corridor the cleanliness is the finding, not the clearance. And because every individual flag has an innocent explanation, evasion is never found in one place. It is found at the joins, the message against the invoice, the invoice against the vessel's track, the company against its own claimed business. Hold that frame. Every typology ahead is one of three disguises, and every red flag is that disguise leaving a trace."

### Step 1 (setup) `Evidence item 1: the perimeter argument`
evidence.observed: "The platform matched trades between its US users and its users in comprehensively sanctioned jurisdictions, Iran prominently. Each matched trade put a US user on one side and a sanctioned-jurisdiction user on the other. The platform's position was that as a virtual-asset business it was a new kind of thing, outside the sanctions obligations this course has built."
evidence.source: "The public record of the plea and settlement documents."
evidence.inference: "The whole matter turns on one question asked before any figure is priced: does the technology change the obligation?"
evidence.confidence: "High; the cross-matching of US and sanctioned-jurisdiction users and the platform's perimeter position are documented in the resolution record."
decision.prompt: "A colleague frames the platform's defence: 'It is a crypto exchange, not a bank. The obligations you have spent two lessons on attach to regulated financial institutions clearing payments. The technology here is genuinely different, so the perimeter does not reach it.' What is the disciplined read?"
- a (CORRECT): "Inside the perimeter. Sanctions obligations attach to the function, exchanging and transmitting value, not to the technology, and FATF Recommendation 15 makes a virtual-asset service provider a covered institution. Each trade matched between a US user and a sanctioned-jurisdiction user is a US sanctions touchpoint whatever the asset class."
- b: "Outside until a party is designated. No user here is on any list, so there is nothing for a screen to catch, and the exchange's user base is its own compliance housekeeping rather than a sanctions exposure the perimeter reaches."
- c: "Inside only where it is licensed. The perimeter reaches the platform at the points it has voluntarily registered with US regulators; absent a US authorisation to hook onto, US sanctions law has no purchase on an offshore exchange."
- d: "A home-supervisor matter. The platform is incorporated offshore, so any exposure runs to its own jurisdiction's authorisation regime and not to US sanctions law, which governs US banks and their clearing, not foreign crypto venues."
decision.explanation: "Sanctions law follows what a business does, not what it is built on. Recommendation 15 and its Interpretive Note place virtual-asset service providers squarely inside the AML/CFT and targeted-financial-sanctions perimeter, and the US charges operationalised the same point through IEEPA and the Bank Secrecy Act: the obligation attaches to the function of exchanging and transmitting value. Every trade the platform matched between a US user and a user in a comprehensively sanctioned jurisdiction is a US touchpoint, which is why 'no list hit' is not a defence but the typology itself, evasion is engineered to screen clean, and the OFAC exposure is strict-liability civil regardless of intent. The licence and home-supervisor readings both mistake a floor for a perimeter: registration and foreign authorisation are additional obligations, not the outer edge of US sanctions reach over conduct that touches US persons."
reveal: "The perimeter argument was not a side point; it was the product. The platform was built to sit outside the obligation, and this matter is where that architecture was priced. The technology was different. The obligation was not."

### Step 2 (escalation, MENS-REA danger zone) `Evidence item 2: the willfulness line`
evidence.observed: "Two things sit on the file together. The geolocation and compliance controls that would have excluded sanctioned-jurisdiction users were deliberately weakened or left circumventable. And the platform's own internal communications acknowledged that users from sanctioned jurisdictions were present and described managing those controls around growth rather than around the law."
evidence.source: "The public record of the plea and settlement documents."
evidence.inference: "Weak controls are one fact. What the institution's own records show it knew is a different fact. The two do not carry the same legal weight, and this step is about telling them apart."
evidence.confidence: "High; both the control weakness and the internal acknowledgements are documented findings of the resolution."
decision.prompt: "A colleague says the controls were so deficient that the deficiency itself proves the platform knew, so this is plainly willful. Where does the willfulness in this matter actually come from?"
- a (CORRECT): "From the records, not the weakness. Weak controls alone are an OFAC apparent-violation exposure under a strict-liability civil standard and do not by themselves establish knowledge. What converts this into a willfulness finding is the platform's own documented acknowledgement that it knew sanctioned-jurisdiction users were present and chose to manage the controls around growth rather than law."
- b: "From the weak controls alone. A control set this deficient could only be deliberate, so the state of the controls is itself the proof of willful blindness, and no separate evidence of what the institution knew is needed to establish it."
- c: "From neither yet. Willfulness cannot attach to an institution without an individual's admission or conviction first, so until a named person pleads there is only a civil exposure and nothing willful on the entity to find."
- d: "From nowhere, because it does not matter. OFAC liability is strict, so intent plays no part anywhere in this matter, and the willfulness question simply does not arise on any part of the file."
decision.explanation: "This is the discrimination the whole course keeps returning to. Run the two tracks apart. The OFAC settlement is civil and strict-liability: apparent violations, no intent element, and weak controls that let prohibited trades through are enough to price it. The criminal charges, the Bank Secrecy Act program failure and the IEEPA violations, do turn on willfulness, and a control failure alone does not supply it; a bad control can be negligence. What supplies willfulness is the institution's own account of why the control stayed bad: records acknowledging the sanctioned-jurisdiction users and describing the controls as managed around growth rather than law. That is the willfulness engine this course has seen from U.S. Bancorp's testing to Standard Chartered's customer files. Option b is the trap, letting the deficiency stand in for knowledge; option c is wrong because corporate willfulness is read from the entity's own records, not gated on an individual plea; option d confuses the civil leg's strict liability for the whole matter, when willfulness is exactly what the criminal charges required."
reveal: "The controls were the exposure. The communications were the proof. The willfulness in this matter was not inferred from how bad the controls were, it was read directly from the institution's own account of why they stayed that way."
